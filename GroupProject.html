Introduction & Problem Statement

The Leverage Effect

Volatility in financial markets is far from constant — it clusters, spikes during crises, and decays slowly during calm periods. A further, well-documented asymmetry makes equity volatility modelling especially rich: negative returns tend to cause larger increases in future volatility than positive returns of the same magnitude. This phenomenon is known as the leverage effect, first formalised by Black (1976) and Christie (1982).

The intuition behind the leverage effect has two main channels. The financial-leverage channel argues that a fall in equity value raises the firm's debt-to-equity ratio, increasing the riskiness of equity and hence its return variance. The volatility-feedback channel (Campbell & Hentschel, 1992) adds that any news raising expected future volatility must reduce the current stock price to compensate investors for greater risk. Both channels predict a negative correlation between returns and changes in volatility.

The standard GARCH(1,1) of Bollerslev (1986) captures volatility clustering through a symmetric quadratic response: only the magnitude ($\varepsilon_{t-1}^2$) enters the variance equation. Two frameworks address this deficiency: GJR-GARCH (Glosten, Jagannathan & Runkle, 1993) adds a multiplicative dummy for negative shocks, while EGARCH (Nelson, 1991) models the logarithm of conditional variance and includes both the magnitude and sign of standardised innovations via a dedicated leverage coefficient $\gamma$.

Research Question & Objectives

"Is there statistically significant evidence of the leverage effect in daily portfolio returns, and does EGARCH outperform symmetric ARIMA-GARCH in capturing conditional volatility dynamics?"

We address this by: (1) verifying stationarity and ARCH effects; (2) processing Multi-Asset Portfolio Uploads (CSV/Excel) and normalizing currencies; (3) analyzing Correlation Matrices and Fat-Tailed Value at Risk; (4) fitting GARCH and EGARCH models by Gaussian MLE; and (5) testing the significance of the leverage parameter $\gamma$.

Stylized Facts & Exploratory Analysis

3.1 Non-Normality

Equity return distributions are characteristically leptokurtic (fat-tailed) and negatively skewed — both violating normality. The Jarque-Bera test combines skewness $S$ and excess kurtosis $K$:

$$JB = \frac{n}{6}\!\left(S^2 + \frac{(K-3)^2}{4}\right) \sim \chi^2(2) \text{ under } H_0: \text{normality}$$
3.2 Autocorrelation & ARCH Effects

Although daily returns are largely unpredictable (consistent with weak-form efficiency), their squares exhibit strong autocorrelation — the hallmark of ARCH effects. The ACF of returns (left) shows negligible serial correlation (bars within the 95% Bartlett band at $\pm 1.96/\sqrt{T}$), while the ACF of squared returns (right) shows highly significant autocorrelation at many lags, confirming that while return levels are unpredictable, their volatility is persistent and forecastable.

3.3 Ljung-Box & ARCH-LM Tests

The Ljung-Box Q-statistic at lag $m$ tests $H_0$: no serial correlation up to order $m$, with $Q(m) = n(n+2)\sum_{k=1}^{m}\hat{\rho}_k^2/(n-k) \sim \chi^2(m)$ under $H_0$. Engle's ARCH-LM test regresses $r_t^2$ on its own $p$ lags and computes $\text{LM} = T \cdot R^2 \sim \chi^2(p)$ under $H_0$: no ARCH effects.

§4

Stationarity Analysis

ARIMA models require covariance-stationary input. We apply two complementary tests with opposing null hypotheses to reach a joint conclusion:

  • ADF — $H_0$: unit root. Rejecting provides evidence of stationarity.
  • KPSS — $H_0$: level-stationary. Rejecting provides evidence of non-stationarity.

A series is confidently stationary only when ADF rejects its null and KPSS fails to reject its own. The ADF regression is:

$$\Delta y_t = \alpha + \beta y_{t-1} + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \phi_j \Delta y_{t-j} + \varepsilon_t, \quad H_0: \beta=0 \text{ (unit root)}$$

The KPSS statistic is $\hat{\eta}_\mu = T^{-2}\sum S_t^2 / \hat{\lambda}^2$, where $S_t = \sum_{s=1}^t (y_s - \bar{y})$ and $\hat{\lambda}^2$ is a Newey-West long-run variance estimate.

§5

Model Specification

5.1 ARIMA Mean Equation

With $d=0$ confirmed by stationarity tests, we search over $p \in \{0,1,2,3\}$ by AIC. The best ARIMA order is ….

$$r_t = \mu + \sum_{i=1}^{p}\phi_i r_{t-i} + \varepsilon_t, \quad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{D}(0, \sigma_t^2)$$

Residuals $\hat\varepsilon_t$ from this equation are then passed to the volatility model. All three models below share the same mean equation.

5.2 GARCH(1,1) — Symmetric Benchmark (Bollerslev, 1986)
$$\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha \varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta \sigma_{t-1}^2, \quad \omega>0,\;\alpha,\beta \geq 0,\;\alpha+\beta<1$$

The persistence $\alpha+\beta$ measures shock decay speed. Near-unity persistence is typical for equity indices. The fundamental limitation: the response is purely quadratic in $\varepsilon_{t-1}$, so positive and negative shocks of equal magnitude have identical effects — asymmetry is structurally impossible.

5.3 GJR-GARCH(1,1) — Asymmetric Benchmark (GJR, 1993)
$$\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \bigl(\alpha + \gamma\,\mathbf{1}[\varepsilon_{t-1}<0]\bigr)\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\sigma_{t-1}^2, \quad \alpha+\tfrac{1}{2}\gamma+\beta<1$$

When $\gamma>0$: bad news $(\varepsilon_{t-1}<0)$ has impact $(\alpha+\gamma)\varepsilon^2$ vs $\alpha\varepsilon^2$ for good news — the leverage effect in variance space.

5.4 EGARCH(1,1) — Proposed Model (Nelson, 1991)

EGARCH Volatility formula:

$$\ln(\sigma_t^2) = \omega + \beta \ln(\sigma_{t-1}^2) + \alpha \left| \frac{\epsilon_{t-1}}{\sigma_{t-1}} \right| + \gamma \frac{\epsilon_{t-1}}{\sigma_{t-1}}$$
$$\ln\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \beta\ln\sigma_{t-1}^2 + \alpha\bigl(|z_{t-1}| - \mathbb{E}[|z|]\bigr) + \gamma z_{t-1}, \quad z_t = \varepsilon_t/\sigma_t$$
$$\mathbb{E}[|z|] = \sqrt{2/\pi} \approx 0.7979 \text{ for } z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1), \quad |\beta|<1 \text{ (stationarity)}$$

The leverage coefficient $\gamma$ is the key parameter: if $\gamma < 0$, a negative standardised shock $z_{t-1}<0$ increases log-variance more than a positive shock of the same size. The log-variance specification guarantees $\sigma_t^2>0$ without sign restrictions on $\omega,\alpha,\gamma$.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation
$$\hat\theta = \arg\max_\theta \sum_{t=1}^T \left[-\tfrac{1}{2}\ln\sigma_t^2 - \tfrac{\varepsilon_t^2}{2\sigma_t^2}\right], \quad \text{AIC}=-2\ell(\hat\theta)+2k,\quad \text{BIC}=-2\ell(\hat\theta)+k\ln T$$

Optimisation uses Nelder-Mead (simplex method, no gradient required) — ideal for the non-linear EGARCH log-likelihood. Standard errors are computed via the numerical Hessian at $\hat\theta$.

Model Diagnostics

A well-specified model should produce standardised residuals $\hat z_t = \hat\varepsilon_t/\hat\sigma_t$ that behave like i.i.d. draws. We test this via Ljung-Box on $\hat z_t$ (residual correlation) and on $\hat z_t^2$ (remaining ARCH effects). A $p$-value above 5% in both indicates the model has adequately captured the dynamics.

§8

Model Comparison: VaR Backtesting & Kupiec POF Test

8.1 95% Value at Risk (VaR) Backtesting

Kupiec POF Test Likelihood Ratio:

$$LR_{POF} = -2 \ln \left( \frac{(1-p)^{n-x} p^x}{(1-\hat{f})^{n-x} \hat{f}^x} \right) \sim \chi^2(1)$$

where $x$ = violations, $n$ = total observations, $p = 0.05$, $\hat{f} = x/n$.

Pro Details (Test Interpretation):

  • The p-value Interpretation: Because the p-values in the table are high ($> 0.05$), we fail to reject the null hypothesis that the model's failure rate is correct. In simple terms: "The model is statistically valid."
  • Leverage Effect ($\gamma$): Notice it is empty (—) for standard GARCH because standard GARCH is symmetric and cannot measure it. For EGARCH, a negative value is standard for equity markets (like S&P 500) because it confirms that market "scares" (price drops) cause more volatility than market "booms."
8.2 Conditional Volatility

The three estimated volatility paths are broadly similar but differ in their reaction asymmetry. EGARCH and GJR-GARCH tend to produce higher volatility estimates following market downturns, reflecting the leverage effect.

8.3 News Impact Curves — The Leverage Effect Visualised

The News Impact Curve (Engle & Ng, 1993) plots $\sigma_t^2$ as a function of $\varepsilon_{t-1}$, holding $\sigma_{t-1}^2$ constant at its unconditional mean. The GARCH NIC is a symmetric parabola; the EGARCH NIC has an asymmetric exponential shape — with the left arm (negative shocks) strictly above the right arm when $\hat\gamma < 0$.
